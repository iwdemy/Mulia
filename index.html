01
What & Why

Culture as a Competitive Moat

Strategy can be copied. Culture cannot. The evidence shows that
what people actually believe — not what companies advertise — drives long-term value.

📈
The Evidence
Integrity drives profit.
Advertising values do not.
S&P 500 study: zero correlation between stated values and performance — but a strong link between employee-perceived integrity and long-term results.
Guiso, Sapienza & Zingales · Journal of Financial Economics, 2015
⚙
The Principle
Culture determines
whether strategy lives or dies.
Culture is the deepest layer of organizational life. It decides if strategy gets absorbed — or rejected.
Schein, 2010 · attr. Peter Drucker
02
Culture in Practice

3M vs Norton

Norton started 10x larger. 3M started from a bankrupt mine.
Only one survived — the one that built a culture of experimentation.

The Innovation Engine
$13B revenue · tens of thousands of products
◆ Started from a failed, bankrupt mine
◆ Built the "15% Rule" — free time to experiment
◆ Decentralized structure that rewards initiative
◆ Post-it, Scotch Tape — born from a culture of trying
The Rigid Fortress
✖ Acquired — no longer independent
◆ Started 10x larger than 3M — dominant market leader
◆ Centralized control — no room for exploration
◆ Grew through acquisition, not innovation
◆ Hostile takeover ended the company entirely
Takeaway: A brilliant start means nothing without an evolutionary culture. Experimentation, not strategy, determines the long-term winner.
Collins & Porras (1994). Built to Last.
02
Culture in Practice

Culture as a Cult

Visionary companies share four cult-like characteristics.
They don't just have culture — they are their culture.

Fervently Held Ideology
◆ Core values rarely change
◆ Applied consistently at every level
◆ Non-negotiable bedrock of identity
Tightness of Fit
◆ Absolute cultural fit required
◆ Misaligned people are expelled
◆ No room for ideological ambiguity
Indoctrination
◆ Formal onboarding rituals
◆ Leadership stories reinforce purpose
◆ Ideology instilled, not just stated
Elitism
◆ Employees feel part of something special
◆ Belonging fuels extraordinary effort
◆ Pride as a performance driver
The "Genius of the AND"
Tight ideological control and broad operational autonomy. Visionary companies preserve their core while stimulating progress — never satisfied, always evolving.
Collins & Porras (1994). Built to Last: Successful Habits of Visionary Companies.
